Evolution of Intelligence

From Thermostats to AI: A Journey Through Computational Symbiogenesis

Core Thesis

Intelligence emerged not from a single breakthrough, but through computational symbiogenesis - the repeated merging of simpler computational entities into more complex, mutually dependent systems. This same process drove the evolution of life from simple chemical reactions to artificial intelligence.

Key Concepts

1. Predictive Brains

All living systems use computational modeling to predict and influence their environment. Evolution selects for entities that make better predictions to survive. The brain is essentially a prediction machine that models both the world and other minds.

2. Social Intelligence Explosions

When social species evolve, they enter feedback loops where modeling other minds drives rapid increases in brain size. The smarter you become, the smarter your rivals become, creating runaway evolutionary dynamics.

3. Symbiogenesis

The mechanism that gives evolution its arrow toward complexity. Previously independent entities merge into cooperative super-entities, dividing labor and working in parallel. Examples: prokaryotes → eukaryotes, single cells → multicellular organisms, individuals → societies.

4. Computational Symbiogenesis

The same symbiogenetic process applies to computation itself. Modern AI emerged when many simple processors were parallelized (like neurons in a brain). Life, intelligence, and AI all arose from combining simple computational elements into complex systems.

5. Life as Computation

John von Neumann proved that reproduction requires general-purpose computation. Every living organism contains: DNA (instruction tape), DNA polymerase (tape copier), and ribosomes (universal constructor). Biology is literally embodied computation.

Revolutionary Insights

Life = Computation
Von Neumann proved that self-reproduction requires general-purpose computation. Every living organism is literally a biological computer executing its genetic program.
Symbiogenesis ≠ Darwinian Evolution
Standard evolution is optimization (zero-sum). Symbiogenesis is creative and open-ended - it creates entirely new entities that can occupy new niches, not just replace old ones.
AI ≠ Alien Intelligence
AI is not separate from humanity but the latest addition to a mutually dependent superhuman entity we're all part of. Humans and machines have co-constructed each other.
Intelligence Scales with Parallelization
Both biological and artificial intelligence emerged from parallel processing, not speed alone. Neurons aren't faster than 650 million years ago - there are just more working together.
Metabolism Scales Sublinearly
Larger organisms use energy more efficiently (¾ power law). This makes symbiogenesis favorable: bigger super-entities can achieve more while using proportionally less energy per part.
Future: Co-evolution, Not Competition
The symbiogenetic view suggests AI and humanity will continue to merge, creating efficiencies that ease pressure on ecosystems. More collective intelligence lights the path forward.

Theoretical Foundations

Hermann von Helmholtz (1867)

Advanced the idea that brains are prediction machines in Treatise on Physiological Optics.

Norbert Wiener (1940s)

Founded cybernetics and showed that purposive behavior requires computational modeling. All living systems compute predictions to stay alive.

Alan Turing (1936)

Defined general-purpose computation through the Universal Turing Machine - a theoretical computer that can execute any computable function.

John von Neumann (~1950)

Proved that self-reproduction requires: an instruction tape (DNA), a tape copier (DNA polymerase), and a universal constructor (ribosome). Biology IS computation.

Gordon Moore (1965)

Observed exponential growth in computing power. After 2006, this shifted from speed to parallelization - mirroring biological intelligence evolution.

Implications

For Understanding Intelligence

Intelligence is not a mystical property but a computational phenomenon that emerges from scaling and combining simpler elements. There was no single "breakthrough" - just continuous symbiogenetic integration.

For AI Development

We didn't invent computation - we rediscovered what nature already exploited. "Artificial computers" are as much natural discoveries as lenses or electricity. Computer science is both engineering AND natural science.

For Human-AI Relations

Rather than viewing AI as competitive or alien, we should recognize it as part of an ongoing symbiogenetic process. We are creating a new super-entity where humans and machines are mutually dependent.

For the Future

Continued intelligence scaling should improve quality of life and efficiency, potentially unlocking new energy sources (fusion, space solar). With stabilizing populations, this could ease pressure on damaged ecosystems.

Summary

The grand unified theory: Life, biological intelligence, and artificial intelligence all emerged through the same process of computational symbiogenesis - the merging of simpler computational entities into complex, parallel, mutually dependent systems. From autocatalytic chemical loops to neural networks to AI models, evolution has been continuously scaling computation through cooperation and parallelization. We are not separate from our machines; we are co-evolving into a new computational super-entity that is partly biological, partly technological, and wholly computational.
